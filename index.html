How AI Changed the Way Students Value Their Education

A workshop study with undergraduate Data Science & AI students, mapped across five themes.

Grace Nguyen · BSc Data Science & AI · University of the Arts London
Scroll ↓ · Hover the nodes
01 — Executive Summary

Generative AI use among UK undergraduates is now near-universal, yet the institutional response — in universities, in policy, and in employer behaviour — has lagged behind. For the students completing their degrees in the middle of this shift, a critical question emerges: how has AI changed the way they value their education?

This portfolio investigates that question through a workshop study with twelve undergraduate students on the BSc Data Science and AI programme at UAL. Using thematic analysis of both written workshop responses and focus group discussion, the research identifies five interconnected themes: effort and active involvement as a precondition for genuine learning; authenticity, ownership and pride in human work; critical engagement as a verification burden rather than a labour-saving gain; shifting perceptions of educational value driven by cost, debt and AI together; and AI literacy as simultaneously an opportunity and a threat for graduate prospects.

The findings suggest that students are not passive recipients of AI's effects on higher education. They are actively considering what "value" means across cognitive, personal, financial and labour-market dimensions simultaneously — and this adoption cannot be addressed by AI policy alone.

02 — Responsible Research & Innovation

Ethics across the whole project — research and solution alike

Responsible research and innovation (RRI) ran through the project as a thread: from how data was gathered, to how it is reported here, to how the solution is designed. This section covers both halves — ethics of the research and ethics of the innovation.

Ethics in the research process

The study was approved by the Unit Leader under the UAL Educational Ethics Code of Practice and its four principles — care, social justice, respect for persons, and beneficence — and followed ESRC research-ethics guidance and data-protection duties under the UK Data Protection Act 2018 and UK-GDPR, enforced by the ICO. In practice: voluntary informed consent with a right to withdraw; survey data anonymised at collection and focus-group recordings pseudonymised; storage only on UAL systems; and lecturers deliberately leaving during the focus group so participants could speak freely.

A further decision was made specifically for this public portfolio: all participant evidence is paraphrased rather than quoted. The original consent contemplated anonymised reporting, not publication on a search-indexed website, so direct quotes could risk re-identifying members of a small cohort. The full quoted analysis is kept in the private appendices submitted to the unit leader — applying UK-GDPR's data-minimisation principle to the act of publishing itself.

Ethics in the proposed innovation

Responsible innovation is also built into the solution. The education-route module in Section 07 is structured through the EPSRC AREA framework (Stilgoe, Owen and Macnaghten, 2013): it anticipates the risk that AI dependency erodes earned skill and that unequal literacy widens divides; reflects on authorship, dependency and verification; engages students as co-designers, not subjects; and acts through an assessed, iterable module. The design does not police students' AI use but treats them as capable agents — an ethic of care extended from research into outcome.

03 — Introduction

An education whose terms changed mid-course

In three years, generative AI has moved from curiosity to near-universal presence in UK higher education: HEPI's 2026 survey finds 95% of undergraduates use AI in at least one way, while the share directly including AI-generated text in assessed work has risen to 12%, up from 8% in 2025 and 3% in 2024 (Stephenson and Armstrong, 2026). This adoption coincides with rising fees, a precarious graduate market, and a sector still working out how to teach, assess and value student work in the age of AI.

Research question

This portfolio asks: How has AI changed the way that students value their education? The question spans not just the financial worth of a degree but how students value their own learning, work, critical engagement and graduate prospects — dimensions they treat as interconnected.

Stakeholders and urgency

The primary stakeholders are current undergraduates, navigating a landscape that shifted after they enrolled; international students, paying higher fees, have an especially acute stake. Universities face questions over their value proposition and assessment integrity, while policymakers and employers shape whether the graduate jobs that justify a degree survive. The urgency is twofold: institutional responses have lagged behind adoption, leaving students to judge AI use alone, and this cohort will be the first to enter a labour market visibly reshaped by AI. As future practitioners of that technology, DSAI students' perceptions are an early indicator of how the next generation of AI practitioners will approach the ethics of their own work.

04 — Background Research

Three intersecting debates

The research question sits across three debates: AI's integration into higher education, student perceptions of degree value, and AI's impact on the graduate labour market.

AI in higher education

Since ChatGPT's release in late 2022, Milano, McGrane and Leonelli (2023) argue that integrating large language models into academic practice raises serious operational, pedagogical and ethical risks — plagiarism, the reliability of AI output, and the validity of traditional assessment. The concern is concrete: HEPI found 95% of UK undergraduates use AI in some way, with direct inclusion of AI-generated text in assessed work rising to 12% in 2026, from 3% in 2024 (Stephenson and Armstrong, 2026). For some students AI frees cognitive resources for deeper learning; for others it becomes a crutch that undermines independent skill.

Student perceptions of educational value

Cost increasingly shapes how students value their degrees. Undergraduates starting in 2024/25 are expected to borrow an average of £44,690, with only 56% forecast to repay in full (Department for Education, 2025), and maximum fees rose to £9,535 in 2024 (Department for Education, 2024). Two-thirds of British graduates accrued more loan interest in 2024–25 than they repaid (Times Higher Education, 2026). As AI replicates classroom content, some question whether a degree's academic content justifies its price — while still valuing university socially.

AI and the graduate labour market

The Institute for Public Policy Research estimates up to 8 million UK jobs are at risk from generative AI without intervention, entry-level roles most exposed (IPPR, 2024). Graduate accountancy adverts fell 44% in 2025 as "Big Four" firms cut intake (BM Magazine, 2025), while the Institute for the Future of Work notes AI is raising the value of human-centric skills as routine analysis is automated (IFOW, 2025) — a paradox in which a degree remains a gatekeeper even as its returns grow uncertain.

05 — Methods & Ethics

How the study was designed and conducted

This study investigated how AI has changed the way that students value their education. Twelve undergraduate students from the BSc Data Science and AI programme at UAL participated across two workshop sessions — seven in Session 1 and five in Session 2. The workshop combined a focus group discussion with a written sticky-note exercise, in which participants responded to five open-ended questions about AI and their experience of higher education. Sessions were held in person and recorded via Microsoft Teams, which generated an automated transcript used for analysis.

05 · a — Analysis

Mapping the words: from codes to themes

Workshop data was analysed using thematic analysis. The transcript was first cleaned to remove researcher prompts and irrelevant content, retaining only substantive participant discussion. Written responses and transcript comments were then coded, with codes grouped into broader themes through an iterative review process and consolidated through group discussion.

05 · b — Ethics

Ethical framework and safeguards

Ethical approval was granted by the Unit Leader on 12 March 2026. The study followed the UAL Educational Ethics Code of Practice and its four principles — ethics of care, social justice, respect for persons, and beneficence — alongside UK research ethics guidance from the ESRC and data handling requirements under the UK Data Protection Act 2018 and UK-GDPR, as enforced by the Information Commissioner's Office. All participants gave informed consent and were free to withdraw at any time. Survey data was anonymised at collection; focus group recordings were pseudonymised, retaining only year of study; and all data was stored on UAL-managed systems accessible only to the research team.

In preparing this portfolio for public publication, the decision was taken to paraphrase participant evidence rather than quote it directly. While the ethics application permitted anonymised reporting, the original framing did not contemplate publication on a genuinely public, search-indexed website — and direct quotes could create unintended identifiability risks for participants in a small cohort. Paraphrased thematic summaries appear here; the full quoted analysis is retained in the private appendices submitted to the unit leader, reflecting the data-minimisation principle of UK-GDPR.
06 — Findings

Five interconnected themes

Thematic analysis identified five themes describing how AI has reshaped the way students value their education; the header nodes and the code map above trace the same structure.

01

Effort and active involvement in learning

AI helps learning only when students stay actively involved. Participants drew a sharp line between guided use and outsourcing the thinking, warning that treating AI as a fallback lowers standards and hollows out learning itself.

02

Authenticity, ownership and pride in human work

Participants valued their own work over AI output even when they judged the AI version technically stronger, tying pride to the effort of authorship while dismissing AI writing as stylistically uniform.

03

Critical engagement and the verification burden

AI shifts effort rather than saving it: vague or fabricated output forces students to verify, edit and redo, moving cognitive labour from generation to checking.

04

Shifting perceptions of educational value

Students weighed tuition, debt and AI-replicable content against a degree's worth, while still valuing university for its social and professional networks — a tension international students, paying higher fees, felt most acutely.

05

AI literacy as both opportunity and threat

Literacy was seen as a competitive advantage, set against employers' incentive to cut graduate hiring through automation — viewed as a generationally unfair threat.

Synthesis

Together, the themes show students actively reconsidering what they value about their education — across cognitive, personal, financial and labour-market dimensions at once.

07 — Solutions & Innovation

One route: a critical AI-literacy module for first-year students

This portfolio proposes a single, education-route innovation: a short critical AI-literacy module in the first year of the BSc Data Science and AI programme, co-designed with students. The route follows from the findings — Themes 1 and 3 show students already sense the line between guided and outsourced use and already carry the verification burden, but lack a shared framework for either.

The evidence supports this focus: students tend to have strong functional AI literacy (operating the tools) but weak critical and ethical literacy — evaluating outputs, recognising bias, judging appropriate use — and interventions targeting that dimension remain under-developed (UNESCO, 2024; Open University, 2025). A documented "digital divide" in AI literacy, where better-resourced students outperform peers in critical evaluation, means untaught reliance on AI risks widening inequality — directly echoing this unit's concern with equity.

Why this is responsible innovation: the AREA framework

The module is structured through the EPSRC AREA framework (Stilgoe, Owen and Macnaghten, 2013), around four commitments:

AnticipateNames the risk the findings surface — AI-as-fallback erodes earned skill, and unequal literacy deepens educational divides.
ReflectBuilds in structured reflection on dependency, authorship and verification, not a yes/no compliance question.
EngageCo-designed with students using the same workshop method that produced this research.
ActDelivered as an assessed module with a teach-verify-reflect cycle, piloted and iterated on student feedback.

What the module teaches

Three evidence-led components: (1) guided vs outsourced use — AI as a thinking aid, not an answer machine; (2) verification literacy — checking outputs, spotting fabrication, owning the editing burden; and (3) equity and ethics — the literacy divide, data and consent, and one's responsibilities as a future AI practitioner.

Why education, not policy or a tool: policy guardrails depend on others to enact, and a bespoke verification tool needs resources and maintenance this project cannot evidence. An educational intervention is viable now, owned by the people it serves, backed by implementation literature, and — by closing the critical-literacy gap rather than policing it — treats students as capable agents rather than risks to manage.
08 — Reflection on Inclusive Design

How inclusive was this research, really?

This section reflects on the study's methods through the lens of inclusive design and co-research, drawing on Chapko et al.'s (2020) work with co-researchers with learning disabilities, in which those historically studied became the researchers. Co-research treats expertise as distributed and the process as something designed with, not for, the people it concerns.

Where the workshop approached inclusive principles

The study was conducted by insiders — DSAI students researching DSAI students — so those most affected were also the interpreters. The open-ended format gave participants control over how they framed their experiences, lecturers left during the focus group to reduce power asymmetries, and theme interpretation among fellow students was less hierarchical than a traditional researcher–subject study.

Where it fell short

Participants were not involved in designing the study — question, format and analysis were all set before recruitment — nor in interpreting their own data, and were not consulted on whether the themes captured their meaning. The study also treated participants as a single category, without systematically addressing how AI's impact differs across international students, socioeconomic backgrounds, or prior AI access.

Toward a more inclusive design

A stronger version would redistribute decision-making: inviting students as co-researchers at the design stage, adding member-checking so participants could review the themes, and sampling for difference rather than uniformity. More broadly, it raises a question central to the unit — what does it mean to research students, as a student, inside an institution one depends on? Co-research troubles the researcher's position; here, that destabilisation is productive, forcing attention to power relations traditional methods leave invisible.

09 — Conclusion

Students are not passive recipients

This portfolio set out to investigate how AI has changed the way that students value their education. Through a workshop study with twelve DSAI undergraduates and thematic analysis of written responses and focus group discussion, it identified five interconnected themes — spanning effort and learning, authenticity and ownership, critical engagement, perceptions of value, and graduate prospects.

Taken together, the findings show that students are actively considering what "value" means in higher education, across cognitive, personal, financial and labour-market dimensions at once. This adoption is shaped by AI but not reducible to it: financial pressures, employer behaviour and the social dimensions of university life all contribute. Responses must therefore engage with the full picture rather than treating AI as the sole variable. What this study contributes is a grounded account of how students training in AI themselves understand these changes — perspectives that matter both because they will enter a labour market reshaped by the technologies they study, and because how they value their education will shape how the next generation of AI practitioners approaches the ethics of their own work.
